Research

Research Summary

I am an aquatic molecular ecologist and past graduate of Dalhousie University’s Marine Biology honours program. My research interests primarily focus on monitoring how significant aquatic species, specifically salmonids, respond to introduced environmental stressors via molecular ecology analysis. A heavy focus of my work is the application of environmental DNA and RNA techniques as a medium to quantify the cumulative health of important marine species populations.

My past experiences have focused both on the development and application of molecular biomonitoring tools, both of which were primary focuses of my honours research. I also have extensive experience with non-molecular approaches to study fish populations such as pit tagging, dissection-based analysis, and fish health surveys. I previously worked as a research technician for the Pacific Salmon Foundation, where I contributed to a wide variety of research initiatives aimed to monitor and protect wild pacific salmonid populations.

Currently, I am doing my MSc under the supervision of Dr. Melania Cristescu at McGill University in collaboration with DFO Canada and the Huntsman Marine Science Centre. I am working on using molecular monitoring techniques to track the recent invasion of Didemnum vexillum along the Bay of Fundy.

Ongoing

Monitoring the Novel Invasion of Didemnum Vexillum in the Bay of Fundy

McGill University - Huntsman Marine Science Centre - DFO Canada

Huntsman Marine Science Centre, in partnership with DFO, McGill University and regional collaborators, is leading a three-year study on the invasive colonial tunicate Didemnum vexillum (“sea vomit”). After its novel discovery in the Bay of Fundy, this species poses new risks to local ecosystems and fisheries. Using SCUBA and ROV surveys, fisher-collected samples, eDNA monitoring, and genetic tools, the project aims to map its distribution and trace invasion pathways to inform management and prevention efforts.

Validation and Implementation of eRNA as an Approach to Monitoring Aquatic Invasive Species

McGill University

This project aims to improve monitoring of invasive marine species by combining environmental RNA (eRNA) and DNA (eDNA) techniques. Focusing on the colonial tunicate Didemnum vexillum (“sea vomit”) in Atlantic Canada, the research compares molecular detections with visual survey data to assess how accurately each method reflects the species’ true presence. By evaluating eRNA’s potential to provide more precise, real-time information than eDNA, the project seeks to advance early detection and management tools for marine invasions.

Understanding Juvenile Chinook Salmon Survival Using Pit Tagging

Pacific Salmon Foundation - Ha’oom Fisheries Society - DFO Canada

In collaboration with Ha’oom Fisheries Society, DFO, and Nuu-chah-nulth Nations, the Pacific Salmon Foundation is investigating why West Coast Vancouver Island Chinook salmon experience poor early-ocean survival. Using PIT-tag tracking and genomic “Fit-Chip” analysis, the project links stress and pathogen signals to individual survival outcomes, guiding recovery efforts for this culturally and ecologically vital species.
